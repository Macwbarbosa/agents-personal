## About This Discussion Paper

The SBTi introduced its first guidelines for science-based target (SBT) setting, including initial requirements for scope 3 target setting, almost a decade ago. This discussion paper offers an overview of the status and current practices of scope 3 target setting among companies with SBTi-validated targets. It discusses the challenges and opportunities associated with scope 3 target setting and explores potential solutions to enhance the effectiveness and impact of value chain decarbonization.

This paper is **informative in nature and does not include any preliminary or definitive requirements, guidelines or other normative elements** that companies must adopt to be validated by the SBTi. It serves as a research input into the process for revising the SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard, announced in May 2024. This revision will adhere to the Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for the Development of SBTi Standards, which includes stages of research, drafting, public consultation, pilot testing, redrafting, approval by the independent Technical Council and adoption by the SBTi Board of Trustees.

*~230 organizations were consulted, covering major industries and geographies. However, due to the inherent sampling bias of the SBTi's outreach, 85% of companies surveyed either had a validated science-based target or were committed to setting a target with the SBTi.*

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### The Importance of Scope 3 Target Setting

In 2015, the Paris Agreement was signed by 196 parties, codifying the world's collective aim to limit global warming to well-below 2°C and agreeing to pursue more ambitious efforts to keep warming to 1.5°C. In 2018, the IPCC released a special report outlining the drastic differences between 1.5°C and 2°C of warming. The report confirmed that our primary aim must be to limit warming to 1.5°C with little to no overshoot.

The IPCC special report on "Global Warming of 1.5°C" confirmed that we must limit warming, with little to no overshoot, to **1.5°C**.

Scope 3 emissions include indirect emissions within a company's value chain. The SBTi believes that scope 3 target setting can serve as a powerful mechanism to integrate our global climate goals into the core of the economy — specifically, into what companies procure and how they generate revenue.

### Purpose of This Paper

The SBTi is undertaking its first major revision of the Corporate Net-Zero Standard (CNZS). Version 1.0 of this standard was released in October 2021. The purpose of this paper is to set out the challenges and opportunities with scope 3 target setting and share the SBTi's initial thinking on the potential changes being explored. This paper has been developed as part of the research phase of the CNZS V2.0 revision process.

### Overview of Scope 3 Target Setting in SBTi Standards

By the end of 2023, **4,205 companies and financial institutions had SBTi-validated targets.** Counted together with companies with commitments to set targets, this represents **39% of the global economy by market capitalization.** Approximately **~97% of companies included scope 3 emissions in their targets.**

Companies can choose from various methods to address scope 3 emissions, including:
- Reducing absolute emissions (**64%** of validated targets)
- Engaging suppliers or customers to set science-based targets (**14%**)
- Decreasing physical intensity (**11%**) or economic intensity (**5%**)
- Using a sectoral decarbonization approach (**6%**)

The current approach allows companies to prioritize emission reductions through target boundaries that address at least **67% of their scope 3 emissions for near-term targets** and a minimum of **90% for long-term targets.**

### Challenges in Scope 3 Target Setting and Implementation

50% of respondents to SBTi's 2023 Survey self-reported to be "off track" for delivering their scope 3 targets.

Five key challenges:

1. **Aggregated emissions metric:** Using aggregated scope 3 emissions as the primary metric has limitations due to variability in GHG accounting methods, reliance on secondary data and the lack of nuance in addressing the very distinct nature of emissions sources in corporate GHG inventories, including the different time periods in which they occur, the measurement approaches required and the specific mitigation levers available.

2. **Target-setting methods:** Current methods, that often project a linear change in emissions or emissions intensity, face challenges given the dynamic nature of value chain emissions and distinct nature of value chain activities.

3. **Target-setting boundaries:** The requirement to cover a minimum percentage of scope 3 emissions (67% near-term, 90% long-term) can lead to misleading target formulation, exclusion of critical emissions and ambiguity about the transition from near- to long-term target boundaries.

4. **Levels of influence:** Implementing scope 3 targets can be challenging due to the variable capacity of companies to influence and mitigate different emissions sources within their value chains.

5. **Progress measurement:** Measuring progress towards decarbonization is complex due to data limitations, emissions volatility and the challenge of linking mitigation actions directly to GHG inventory changes.

### Options Under Consideration to Enhance Scope 3 Target Setting

**1. A more comprehensive set of tools to manage value chain emissions:**
- Explore a wider range of metrics beyond aggregate scope 3 emissions (tCO₂e) to include outcome-based metrics that measure alignment of upstream (procurement) and downstream (revenue generation) activities with global climate goals.
- By supplementing GHG emission targets with alignment targets based on metrics that demonstrate actions and outcomes aligned with global climate goals, companies and stakeholders can more effectively take action, assess and communicate progress.
- Complement targets with policies that may be effective in driving action towards achieving targets.

**2. A more nuanced approach to target-setting boundaries:**
- Explore alternatives to a blanket target boundary approach, including a more nuanced approach that prioritizes action on the most climate-relevant activities.
- Identification of climate-relevant activities proposed using three parameters: (a) magnitude of emissions; (b) exposure to activities in high-climate-impact sectors; and (c) risks of locking-in future emissions.

**3. Exploring the role of influence:**
- Influence over emissions from suppliers or users of sold products is a key challenge for companies delivering on scope 3 targets.
- Two possible options: (a) prioritization of emissions sources — assessment of influence used to exclude from target boundary emissions sources companies have no/limited influence over; (b) determining differentiated interventions — different interventions required depending on level of influence.
- Both options carry risks due to the subjective nature of determining influence.

### Exploring the Role of Certification and Environmental Attribute Certificates

As companies move from target setting to target implementation, more clarity is needed on how to substantiate claims of progress. Five potential scenarios:

1. **Scenario 1:** Use of commodity certificates from value chain activities
2. **Scenario 2:** Use of commodity certificates from sources with lower or no value chain traceability (interim solution with guardrails)
3. **Scenario 3:** Use of carbon credits from mitigation activities within the value chain to substantiate value chain emission reduction claims
4. **Scenario 4:** Use of carbon credits to support neutralization of residual emissions
5. **Scenario 5:** Use of carbon credits to support beyond value chain mitigation (BVCM)

**Critical note:** SBTi standards require that carbon credits are **not counted** as emission reductions toward the progress of companies' science-based targets.

**BVCM definition:** Beyond Value Chain Mitigation — companies delivering BVCM through purchase and retirement of high-quality carbon credits and direct investments. BVCM acts as a **supplement** to value chain abatement activities, not a **substitute**.

### Operationalizing the Proposals — Five-Step Process (Figure 7)

The proposed framework centers on: *Set a company level commitment and strategy to reach net-zero emissions across the value chain by **2050***

- **Step 1:** Measure and disclose greenhouse gas emissions across the value chain
- **Step 2:** Identify and prioritize climate-relevant emissions sources
- **Step 3:** Establish targets and policies to mitigate climate-relevant emissions sources
- **Step 4:** Implement actions to achieve targets and take responsibility for emissions not yet included within target boundary
- **Step 5:** Measure effectiveness of interventions and communicate progress

The five steps are **cyclical** — companies periodically review their value chain net-zero strategies.

### Areas for Further Work

- Research methods to determine benchmarks for outcome-based metrics aligned with global climate goals.
- Feasibility of introducing and standardizing the concept of influence in target-setting.
- Use of different tools, including certification and taxonomies to define science-based benchmarks.
- Use of carbon credits from abatement activities within the value chain to substantiate value chain emission reduction claims.
- Effectiveness of different EACs to drive mitigation and transformation outcomes consistent with reaching global climate goals when used under a book and claim model.
- Rigorous examination of the credibility of potential claims related to value chains enabled by CNZS V2.0.
- In-depth risk assessment of areas for improvement in the scope 3 target-setting framework.
- Further research of equity considerations in the design of scope 3 target-setting requirements.

### Call for Stakeholder Feedback

The SBTi's goal is to deliver a more actionable, transparent and impactful framework that supports its mission to drive science-based climate action in the corporate sector consistent with 1.5°C and net-zero. The SBTi encourages stakeholders to provide feedback on the proposed approaches and preliminary options to improve the value chain claims framework.

## Assessing Current Scope 3 Target Setting Practices

### Overview of Scope 3 Target Setting in SBTi Standards

Over the past decade, scope 3 target setting has evolved from being a leading practice into a common practice. By the end of 2023, 4,205 companies and financial institutions had set SBTi-validated targets and companies with science-based targets or commitments represented 39% of the global economy by market capitalization.

The current approach under SBTi standards provides some flexibility in this prioritization process, allowing companies to address a minimum of 67% of their scope 3 emissions for near-term targets, increasing their mitigation efforts towards 90% coverage in the long term (by 2050).

**Figure 2 — Methodologies used in setting scope 3 targets:**
- Absolute contraction: 64%
- Engagement: 14%
- Physical intensity: 11%
- Sectoral decarbonization approach: 6%
- Economic intensity: 5%

### Challenges in Scope 3 Target Setting and Implementation

Five key challenges identified:
1. The use of aggregated scope 3 emissions as the primary metric for managing value chain impacts.
2. The limitations of current target-setting methods.
3. The approach used to define target boundaries.
4. Limited nuance around levers and levels of influence.
5. The difficulty in assessing and communicating progress over time.

**Key Challenge 1 — Use of Aggregated Scope 3 Emissions as Primary Metric:**
- Optionality in GHG accounting and calculation approaches: flexibility and optionality result in limited comparability of corporate GHG inventories between companies and consistency over time.
- Limited availability of reliable data: vast majority of companies estimate value chain emissions using secondary data (average emission factors from databases); only a small percentage estimate from primary data sources.
- Usability of an aggregated emissions metric: aggregating different scope 3 categories into a single metric obscures the distinct nature of activities and emissions sources. For example, upstream supply chain emissions normally occur in the past, and downstream emissions expected from the further processing, use and disposal of products occur in the future.

**Key Challenge 2 — Limitations with Current Target-Setting Methods:**
- Difficulty in defining science-based benchmarks for scope 3.
- Metric volatility: Scope 3 emissions can be highly dynamic — even in a data-perfect world, aggregate scope 3 emissions are in constant change for several reasons including market-share variations, changes in product portfolios, changes in suppliers and changes in materials.
- Limited nuance for net-zero aligned activities: Methods based on comparing aggregate scope 3 emissions against global emissions benchmarks fail to consider the existing level of net-zero alignment of specific activities within value chains.

**Key Challenge 3 — Limitations with Current Approach to Target-Setting Boundaries:**
The current SBTi approach requires companies to include a minimum of 67% of their scope 3 emissions within the scope 3 target boundary for near-term targets and a minimum of 90% for long-term targets. Unintended consequences:
- Potentially misleading target formulation
- Exclusion of high-climate-impact activities
- Lack of clarity on how to increase the target boundary over time

**Key Challenge 4 — Limited Nuance Around Levels of Influence:**
"Influence" = the capacity of an entity or actor to effect or exert change over emissions-related decisions, actions and outcomes within the value chain. An average internal combustion engine (ICE) car, for example, emits roughly 80% of its emissions over its lifetime whilst the engine is in use. The current approach to scope 3 target setting treats all emissions sources equally, without acknowledging the varying levels of influence and potential levers available.

**Key Challenge 5 — Challenges in Assessing Progress Towards Value Chain Decarbonization Targets:**
Given limited access to primary data and the volatility of scope 3 emissions, there is significant complexity in measuring value chain decarbonization and progress against targets. A more extended discussion on challenges around scope 3 target setting can be found in Annex I.

## Options Under Consideration to Enhance Scope 3 Target Setting

### A More Comprehensive Set of Tools to Address Value Chain Emissions

The SBTi is exploring how to expand focus beyond traditional metrics like aggregate scope 3 emissions (tCO₂e) to include new metrics that assess the alignment of an organization's procurement and revenue generation activities with global climate goals.

*The SBTi believes that scope 3 target-setting can be a **key lever to catalyze transformation** of corporate value chains consistent with limiting warming to 1.5°C.*

**Metrics to assess value chain alignment with global climate goals:**
- "Metric" = a quantifiable indicator used to assess, manage, compare and communicate the past, current or intended climate-related performance of an organization.
- Metrics can be expressed in terms of impact (e.g. GHG emissions released into the atmosphere) or outcome (e.g. percentage of electricity sourced from zero-carbon sources).

**Upstream emissions outcome-based metric:** Operational expenditure directed towards entities, activities and commodities that have achieved a level of emissions performance compatible with reaching net-zero emissions at the global level.

**Downstream emissions outcome-based metric:** Revenue derived from entities, activities, products and services that have achieved a level of emissions performance compatible with reaching net-zero emissions at the global level.

**Figure 3** illustrates how value chain alignment (outcome-based metrics) and value chain emissions (impact-based metrics) relate over time (2025–2050): as emissions decrease, alignment increases.

**Introduce additional interventions:**
- Alignment targets and policies could supplement or provide an alternative to value chain emission reduction targets.
- Alignment targets for procurement: incentivize increased share of procurement from entities, activities and commodities that demonstrate emissions performance compatible with achieving net-zero emissions by 2050.
- Downstream alignment targets: incentivize an increase in revenue derived from products, services or entities that meet emissions performance aligned with net-zero goals.

Products particularly relevant for downstream alignment:
- Fossil fuel products and feedstocks (e.g. coal products, petroleum products, natural gas, crude oil)
- Products that rely on fossil fuel combustion during use phase (e.g. internal combustion vehicles, fossil fuel fired equipment such as furnaces, kilns, boilers, generators)
- Products that consume electricity during use phase (e.g. appliances, electric vehicles, electric heaters, air conditioners)
- Products that emit GHGs during use phase (e.g. refrigerators, nitrogen fertilizers)

**Company policies** — examples:
- Policies to address supply chain emissions
- Travel and transport policies
- Product and service policies
- Policies for climate policy and lobbying
- Sector-specific decarbonization policies

### Revise Target-Setting Boundary Approach

Three parameters being considered for impact-based prioritization:
- **Magnitude:** Volume of emissions released by a given activity in the value chain (relative or absolute terms).
- **Activities in high-climate-impact sectors:** Exposure to energy-intensive and land-use-intensive sectors.
- **Activities with high risk of emissions lock-in:** Infrastructure with a lifetime incompatible with achieving net-zero emissions by 2050.

Four options being explored for near-term target boundary (Table 4):
- **Option 1:** Align near-term boundary with net-zero target boundary requirements (90%), supplemented by climate-relevant emissions sources if necessary.
- **Option 2:** Retire percentage target boundary concept. Focus on climate-relevant emissions sources with transparent justification.
- **Option 3:** Use a 67% near-term target boundary and 90% long-term target boundary as minimum threshold, supplemented by climate-relevant emissions sources.
- **Option 4:** Select a minimum of the two most relevant scope 3 categories. (According to LSEG analysis, two most material scope 3 categories cover at least 72% — on average 81% — of overall scope 3 emissions per sector.)

### Consideration of Influence in the Target-Setting Framework

Two possible options:
- **Prioritization of emissions sources:** Assessment of influence used to prioritize which emissions sources should be addressed. Emissions sources over which companies have no or limited influence might be excluded from the target boundary.
- **Determining differentiated interventions:** Assessment of influence informs which interventions companies use to address emissions.

Both options carry potential risks, in part due to the high degree of subjectivity in defining thresholds for "reasonable" versus "limited influence."

## Exploring the Role of Environmental Attribute Certificates in Addressing Value Chain Emissions

### What Are Environmental Attribute Certificates?

Environmental attribute certificates (EACs) encompass a diverse range of instruments that certify and communicate specific environmental or sustainability attributes of a given activity or commodity. Generally, two broad categories:

**Commodity certificates:** Instruments that certify and convey sustainability information about the production process of different commodities. Provide verified data on the environmental and/or social performance of a commodity in conformance with a specific sustainability standard.
- Energy carriers (e.g. hydrogen gas, liquid fuels, electricity)
- Agricultural commodities (e.g. beef, soy, coffee, cotton)
- Industrial commodities (e.g. steel, cement, aluminium)

**Carbon credits:** Tradable instruments representing the outcome of a mitigation activity (project, program, or intervention), typically quantified in tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent. Different nomenclatures: e.g. Verra issues Verified Carbon Units (VCUs), Gold Standard issues Gold Standard Verified Emission Reductions (GS-VERs).
- Avoided emission credits
- Emission reduction credits
- Carbon sequestration credits

### Importance of Traceability

The credibility and robustness of sustainability-related claims depend on the integrity of the standard used and the model employed to establish traceability.

Chain of custody models: identity preservation and physical segregation (most robust) → physical separation → mass balance → book and claim (least traceability — certificates are unbundled from the physical flow of commodities).

The Advanced Indirect Mitigation platform (AIM) categorizes traceability into three main categories: physical association, close association, sector association.

### Potential Use Cases of Certification and EACs in Corporate Mitigation Strategies

**Scenario 1 — Use of commodity certificates from value chain activities:** Certification systems might support value chain mitigation claims by allowing buyers to prove that their sourced commodities meet emissions standards aligned with global climate goals. A robust chain of custody is crucial to enable traceability.

**Scenario 2 — Use of commodity certificates from sources with lower or no value chain traceability:**
An interim solution with clear guardrails (e.g. time, volume). Limited to high-quality, high-impact certificates.

*Table 1 — Potential risks for unbundled certificates:*
- Impact dilution
- Finance dilution
- Mitigation deterrence
- Emissions lock-in
- Misleading claims

**Scenario 3 — Use of carbon credits from mitigation activities within the value chain to substantiate value chain emission reduction claims:**
*Important: SBTi standards require that carbon credits are **not counted** as emission reductions toward the progress of companies' science-based targets.* This scenario explores the possibility of carbon credits to support value chain emission reduction claims if they represent emission abatement from activities traceable to the company's value chain, and that these mitigation outcomes are fungible with corporate GHG emissions inventory.

Key areas requiring further exploration:
- Defining and standardizing value chain traceability.
- Accounting adjustments when baseline emissions are estimated using secondary data.
- Adjusting for activities issuing multiple certificates.
- Adjustments needed to prevent double claiming.

**Scenario 4 — Use of carbon credits to support neutralization of residual emissions:**
The SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard requires companies to neutralize the climate impact of scientifically-defined residual emissions at their net-zero target year by permanently removing and storing carbon from the atmosphere. Scientifically-defined residual emissions are those that remain after the implementation of all possible mitigation measures considered in pathways that limit warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot, covering scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions.

Key concepts being explored:
- Matching emissions type with storage type (biogenic or geologic)
- Matching atmospheric lifetime with storage timescale (physical equivalence)
- Establishing fungibility between removal methods (economic equivalence)

**Scenario 5 — Use of carbon credits to support beyond value chain mitigation (BVCM):**
BVCM = contribution to the broader societal shift towards net-zero. Companies can deliver BVCM through purchase and retirement of high-quality carbon credits and direct investments. The traditional practice of offsetting is not accepted under SBTi standards. BVCM acts as a **supplement** to value chain abatement activities, not a substitute.

*Table 2 — Potential risks for BVCM carbon credits:*
- Integrity risks
- Impact risks
- Finance dilution risks
- Mitigation deterrence risks
- Emissions lock-in risks
- Misleading claims risks

**Figure 6** illustrates how companies can be incentivized to abate scope 3 emissions, neutralize residual emissions, and finance mitigation outside their value chain. The minimum share of emissions that companies are expected to address through abatement within their value chain starts at **67%** and gradually increases to **90% by 2050**, in line with the SBTi Corporate Net Zero Standard. By the net-zero target year (2050), **90% of the emissions are expected to have been abated**, with the remaining **10% counterbalanced through appropriate removal activities.** The shift towards neutralization activities starts from 2030.

## Operationalizing the Proposals

This section presents a description of how the concepts outlined could potentially be combined into a process for value chain transformation consisting of five key steps (Figure 7). These steps are **not intended to be preliminary or definitive guidelines or requirements** and are subject to further deliberation as part of the SOP for Development of SBTi Standards.

### Step 1: Measure and Disclose GHG Emissions Across the Value Chain

**Step 1.1 — Develop a GHG emissions inventory as per the Corporate Net-Zero Standard:**
- Develop a scope 3 inventory based on the GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Accounting and Reporting Standard and the Scope 3 Calculation Guidance.
- Ensure that all relevant categories and emissions sources specified within the minimum boundary in Table 5.4 of the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard are included.
- Break down emissions inventory into individual GHGs, if relevant for the sector (e.g. fossil fuel production and distribution, agriculture, waste management).
- Estimate, describe and disclose any exclusions from the inventory.
- Undertake third-party GHG verification by an independent third party (recommended).
- Publicly report the GHG inventory annually.

### Step 2: Identify and Prioritize Climate-Relevant Emissions Sources

Three parameters to assess climate impacts:

**Step 2.1 — Identify the emissions sources that contribute to the GHG inventory:**
- Break down the inventory by emissions source (commodities, products, services, activities).
- The SBTi currently defines a **significance threshold of 5%**, which may offer a useful rule of thumb.

**Step 2.2 — Assess inventory based on magnitude of emissions:**
- Prioritize emission hotspots; rank from highest to lowest.
- Potential thresholds:
  - Absolute: Emissions (e.g. ≥10,000 CO₂e) or Financial (e.g. ≥$25,000 spend or revenue)
  - Relative: Emissions (e.g. ≥5% of total scope 3) or Financial (e.g. ≥5% spend or revenue)

**Step 2.3 — Assess inventory against activities in high-climate-impact sectors:**
- Undertake a check to identify activities in high-climate-impact sectors and eligible exclusions.

**Step 2.4 — Assess inventory against activities with high risk of emissions lock-in:**
- Identify activities that, if left unaddressed, could result in the lock-in of high-emission infrastructure.

**Step 2.5 — Establish target boundary:**
- Determine where to focus efforts for near-term scope 3 targets.
- Four options being considered (see Table 4 in document).

**Figure 8** illustrates high-level example of how a company might assess value chain emissions against the three parameters (2.2 Magnitude, 2.3 High-climate-impact sector, 2.4 Risk of emissions lock-in) to identify climate-relevant emissions sources.

### Step 3: Establish Targets and Policies to Mitigate Climate-Relevant Emissions Sources

**Step 3.1:** Set a long-term emission reduction target for 2050 latest on scope 3 emissions based on current SBTi methods using the aggregated scope 3 metric.

**Step 3.2:** Set near-term targets on climate-relevant emissions sources — select appropriate target-setting methods (emissions-based and/or alignment-based).

**Step 3.3:** Establish relevant policies that address value chain net-zero transformation:
- Policies to address supply chain emissions
- Travel and transport policies
- Product and service policies
- Policies for climate policy and lobbying
- Sector-specific decarbonization policies

**Step 3.4:** Develop a clear action plan to support the net-zero commitment — avoid adverse climate impacts; encourage responsible business practices; mitigate and remediate unavoidable impacts; continuously improve the quality of disclosed data.

### Step 4: Implement Actions to Achieve Targets and Take Responsibility for Emissions Not Yet Included Within Target Boundary

**Step 4.1:** Develop and implement action plans to prevent or mitigate actual or potential climate impacts:
- Assign responsibility; consider available levers; support/collaborate with business partners; use leverage to effect positive change; build collective leverage.

**Step 4.2:** Take responsibility for emissions not yet prioritized:
- Options: financing mitigation activities outside of value chain (e.g. purchase and retirement of high-quality carbon credits), direct investments.
- **IMPORTANT:** Financing mitigation activities outside of the company's value chain acts as a **supplement** to value chain abatement activities within the target boundary, **not as a substitute.**

### Step 5: Measure Effectiveness of Interventions and Communicate Progress

**Step 5.1 — Measurement and reporting:**
- Clear and comprehensive public annual reporting of progress.
- Disclose justification of approach to setting targets (methodologies and significant assumptions).
- If applicable, disclose why excluded emissions sources are not addressed.
- Disclose a transition plan, including information on actions planned.

**Step 5.2 — Continuous reassessment:**
- Embed continuous reassessment of emission source to ensure any emissions sources previously identified as not relevant, but that are now relevant, are covered.
- Ensure robustness and transparency related to ambition and performance throughout the net-zero journey.

## Conclusions and Next Steps

### Conclusion

Since the SBTi first introduced initial requirements for scope 3 target setting almost a decade ago, widespread adoption of scope 3 targets has helped to drive a paradigm shift towards companies taking responsibility for emissions across their entire value chains. This paper has set out the key challenges associated with the existing target-setting framework, which stem primarily from deriving metrics and methods from aggregated scope 3 emissions, which are subject to optionality, volatility and data reliability constraints.

The SBTi encourages stakeholders to provide feedback on the proposed approach and preliminary options to improve the value chain framework.

## Glossary (p. 56–57)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Actions | Activities that are undertaken to ensure that the company delivers against a specific policy objective or a target. |
| Alignment | Consistency with outcomes that are compatible with achieving net-zero emissions by 2050, in line with limiting global warming to 1.5°C by 2100. |
| Emissions sources | Commodities, products, services and activities that release greenhouse gas emissions. |
| Impacts | Long-term effects (direct or indirect, intended or unintended) on the sustainability issue produced as the result of an intervention. |
| Intervention | Policies, targets, and actions that companies put in place to address emissions in their value chain. |
| Metrics | A target-setting metric is a quantifiable indicator to assess, manage, compare and communicate the past, current or intended climate-related performance of an organization. Metrics can be expressed in terms of impact or outcome. Impact-based metrics measure the actual effects or results of an organization's activities on the climate (e.g. greenhouse gas emissions released into the atmosphere). Outcome-based metrics measure the extent to which an organization's strategies, operations, and business model are aligned with global climate goals (e.g. percentage of electricity sourced from zero-carbon sources). |
| Net-zero value chain | A net-zero value chain means that all stages of the value chain — from raw material extraction, through to production and distribution, to product use and disposal — must operate without resulting in the accumulation of GHGs in the atmosphere. Meeting this goal, aligned with the Paris Agreement, requires mitigation in line with pathways that limit global warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot to reach net-zero by 2050. |
| Outcomes | The short-term and medium-term effects of an intervention on the sustainability issue in question. |
| Policies | A set or framework of general objectives and management principles that the company uses for decision-making. |
| Value chain | A value chain encompasses the activities, resources and relationships the undertaking uses and relies on to create its products or services from conception to delivery, consumption and end-of-life. |

## Annex I: Key Challenges Related to Current Target-Setting Methods (pp. 61–73)

### Challenge 1: Aggregated Scope 3 Metric

Over half of disclosed scope 3 values vary at least **20% year-on-year** (LSEG); over a third vary at least **50% year-on-year**. Only **6% average use** of supplier-specific emission factors across all scope 3 categories (Figure 9).

**Figure 10** visualizes retrospective vs. prospective emissions across scope 3 categories — showing data quality and coverage gaps.

The core problem: scope 3 emissions are calculated as a function of both an **emission factor (EF)** and **exposure**, expressed in the formula from Figure 12:

> **Scope 3y = (EFy,1 × Exposurey,1) + (EFy,2 × Exposurey,2) + …**

This means year-on-year changes in scope 3 values may reflect changes in emission factors rather than actual reductions, making it difficult to set meaningful science-based benchmarks.

Business travel (scope 3, category 6) is disclosed by **87% of companies** but represents **under 1% of total emissions** — illustrating that easy-to-measure ≠ most material.

### Challenge 2: Target-Setting Methods

**Figure 11 — Levers by position in value chain:**
- **Upstream:** sourcing policies, contractual cascading, shift to low-carbon materials
- **Midstream:** business travel, remote work, waste minimization, clean energy carriers
- **Downstream:** contractual arrangements, licensee/franchisee arrangements, minimization of energy in sold products, circular business models

### Challenge 3: Scope 3 Boundaries

**Figure 13 — ABC Corporation example:**
- Total scope 3: 100 MtCO₂e
- 67% boundary target covers 67 MtCO₂e
- Stated target: 50% reduction within boundary = 33.5 MtCO₂e abated
- Actual total scope 3 reduction = only **34%** (33 MtCO₂e outside boundary unchanged)

**Figure 14 — XYZ Ltd. example:**
- 70% boundary → 70 MtCO₂e in boundary; 30 MtCO₂e outside unchanged
- Stated 20% reduction = 56 MtCO₂e after
- Actual total scope 3 reduction = only **14%**

**Figure 15 — Automaker example:**
- 80% use phase (vehicle), 7.5% steel, 7.5% other supply chain, 5% other scope 3
- The 67% minimum boundary potentially covers only the use phase, excluding steel — a critical net-zero sector
- Demonstrates how the minimum boundary rule can allow high-lock-in steel supply chain emissions to remain out of scope

**Acme Corporation example (boundary misalignment with long-term trajectory):**
- Total scope 3 footprint: **100,000 tCO₂e**
- Near-term 67% boundary target → total expected scope 3 at 2030 = **79,900 tCO₂e**
- Long-term 90% boundary target → expected reductions at 2030 = **27,000 tCO₂e**
- Near-term reductions = 20,100 tCO₂e, which is **6,900 tCO₂e (26%) lower** than required for long-term trajectory alignment

### Challenge 4: Levels of Influence

Current approach **treats all emissions sources equally**. Company capacity to influence varies by:
- Market power
- Type of relationship
- Relative position in value chain
- Technological barriers

Example: an ICE car emits roughly **80% of emissions over its lifetime whilst the engine is in use** — yet the car manufacturer's ability to influence those emissions depends on consumer behavior, policy and technology adoption.

### Challenge 5: Measuring Progress

**Figure 17** maps the progression: Interventions → Intermediate outcomes → Long-term outcomes → Impacts.

Outcome metrics that connect interventions to impacts are **"not widely used in the current framework."** The gap between intervention (e.g., engaging a supplier) and impact (actual scope 3 reduction) is large and poorly measured.

## Annex II: Value Chain Decarbonization Theory of Change (pp. 74–77)

**IPCC** estimates human activities contributed approximately **1.07°C** of global warming since pre-industrial levels.

**IPCC definition of net-zero GHG emissions:** "condition in which metric-weighted anthropogenic GHG emissions are balanced by metric-weighted anthropogenic GHG removals over a specified period."

**EFRAG definition of value chain:** "the full range of activities, resources and relationships related to the undertaking's business model and the external environment in which it operates."

**GHG Protocol definition of value chain:** "all of the upstream and downstream activities associated with the operations of a company, including the use of sold products by consumers and the end-of-life treatment of sold products after consumer use."

### Metrics to Measure Alignment with Global Climate Goals (p. 81)

For the purpose of this paper, **"alignment"** means consistency with achieving net-zero emissions by 2050, in line with limiting global warming to 1.5°C by 2100.

Aligning value chains with climate goals involves both:
- **Demand-side measures** (procurement activities): incentivize companies to align purchasing of high-climate-impact activities and materials (cement, steel, etc.) with global climate goals
- **Supply-side measures** (revenue generation activities): focus company action on reducing emissions from all products or services that either directly generate emissions in their use, or indirectly generate emissions, to ensure all products and services become aligned over time

The **degree of alignment** = extent to which both upstream procurement and downstream revenue are aligned with 1.5°C outcomes.

The intended final outcome: operational expenditure directed towards, and revenue derived from, entities, activities, commodities, products and services that have achieved a level of emissions performance **compatible with reaching net-zero emissions at the global level.**

What qualifies as "aligned" changes over time as entities and activities must decarbonize along 1.5°C pathways and reach net-zero emission performance level by 2050. Therefore, what it takes to be aligned today will be different from what it takes to be aligned in 2040.

The **percentage of upstream and downstream activities that are aligned** should increase gradually over time to **100% by 2050 at the latest.** As this share increases, it will be reflected as a reduction in overall scope 3 emissions (the impact metric).

Progress against outcome-based alignment can therefore be utilized to **forecast likely changes in impact-based metrics** (i.e. scope 3 emissions), providing early signals of potential shifts in scope 3 emissions.

### Considering Alignment at Entity and Emission Source Levels (p. 82)

Value chain alignment can be assessed at two levels:

**1. Entity level** — alignment of entities (suppliers or customers) with which the company has a business relationship:
- An entity is considered aligned if it has a **credible entity-level ambition consistent with limiting warming to 1.5°C** and is transitioning toward or has reached a net-zero end state in alignment with SBTi Standards
- Assessed based on ambition and eventual progress against targets

**2. Emissions source level** — alignment of specific commodities, products, services and activities comprising the value chain:
- An emissions source is considered aligned if it is transitioning toward or has reached **net-zero lifecycle emissions at a rate consistent with limiting warming to 1.5°C**
- Assessed based on **low-carbon certification** or against **appropriate physical intensity benchmarks** aligned with sector or product specific science-based pathways

**Figure 21 — Value chain alignment at both entity and emissions source level:**
- Upstream (entity-level): upstream business partners, e.g. suppliers — entity-level alignment based on SBT status, transition plan
- Downstream (entity-level): downstream business partners, e.g. customers — entity-level alignment based on SBT status, transition plan
- Upstream (emissions source-level): procurement-related high climate impact emissions sources — activity/commodity-level alignment, based on certification, physical intensity
- Downstream (emissions source-level): revenue-related high climate impact emissions sources — product/service-level alignment, based on certification, physical intensity

- Scenario ②: Beyond tier 1 relationship with high climate impact emissions source (e.g. glass) — **commodity-level alignment may be required**

### Establishing Science-Based Targets Using Outcome Metrics (p. 87)

- Pathways from climate scenarios can be used to set science-based targets defining the **rate of change in emissions** over a given timeframe to reach the desired end state (net-zero by 2050)
- Because scope 3 emissions are a function of both emissions intensity and exposure, there may not be a clear method for defining a science-based rate of change for scope 3 emissions
- A science-based end state is clearer to define: **all value chain activities would need to have achieved net-zero by 2050**
- Interim benchmarks are less obvious — for many outcome metrics (e.g. share of procurement spend going to suppliers with SBTs, or share of high-emitting commodities that are net-zero certified), benchmarks for future performance levels may not be directly derived from climate science; they may need to be informed by other theories of change

**Figure 24 — Example of s-curve model to set rate of change performance benchmarks:**
- Example: percentage of aligned materials embedded into products
- At 2030: x% zero- or low-carbon materials embedded into products
- At 2040: higher percentage (inflection point of s-curve)
- At 2050: **100% zero- or low-carbon materials embedded into products**
- S-curve shape reflects adoption dynamics of zero-carbon transitions

## Annex IV: Prioritizing Activities in High-Climate-Impact Sectors (pp. 88–89)

Certain commodities, products, services and activities that have a **high emissions intensity, and degradation of carbon sinks**, are associated with relatively higher climate impacts and merit greater attention in climate strategies.

The SBTi wants to ensure that activities most important for the global transition to net-zero are addressed; exploring option of assessing value chain emissions against activities **determined to be critical for this transition.**

To enable a clearer understanding of climate impacts, it is useful to break down the GHG inventory at a more granular level, looking at emissions from specific activities and resources that make up each scope 3 category. Example: a company could break down its emissions from business travel (scope 3, category 6) at the activity level into flights, buses and passenger cars.

**Sources identifying high-climate-impact emissions sources:**
- EU Technical Expert Group on Sustainable Finance's **2019 report** defined **nine high-climate-impact sectors** (fn. 78)
- **CDP identifies** a list of high-impact sectors alongside their most relevant scope 3 categories in a 2024 Technical Note
- **Science Based Targets Network** compiled a list of high-impact commodities classified by "material pressures" including climate change

The consultation draft of the **SBTi Financial Institutions Net-Zero Standard (FINZ)** provides a model for identifying and prioritizing high-climate-impact value chain emissions:
- Drawing on the IEA's CO₂ Emissions in 2022 report, the FINZ draft includes "emissions intensive" sectors and activities
- Given their significant contribution to global emissions of approximately **70%**, the FINZ Standard draft requires financial institutions to include any financing activities associated with these sectors within their target boundaries, subject to an exclusion threshold of a contribution of **5% of emissions**
- **Nine sectors included in the FINZ Standard draft:** cement, oil and gas, power generation, steel, automotive, aviation, shipping and buildings

## Annex V: Overview of Environmental Attribute Certificates and Chain of Custody Models (pp. 90–95)

### Commodity Certificates (p. 91)

Some of the most commonly used certificates employed by corporations to substantiate climate-related claims include:

**1. Energy certificates:** certificates for different energy carriers that either convey the emissions profile directly or provide characteristics that help define the emissions profile (e.g. type of feedstock source, or type of technology employed):
- **Electricity:** Renewable electricity certificates or **Guarantees of Origin (GOs)** establish that an amount of electricity has been generated from renewable or other zero emissions sources, helping companies substantiate claims related to zero emissions electricity use
- **Gas:** Certificates such as **Renewable Gas Certificates (RGCs)** or **Green Gas Certificates** verify that gas has been sourced from renewable or low-carbon sources, like biogas or synthetic methane
- **Hydrogen:** Green hydrogen certificates verify that hydrogen has been produced from renewable sources or through low-carbon processes
- **Biofuels:** Biofuel certification establishes that biofuels meet specific sustainability and emissions reduction criteria
- **Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF):** SAF certificates verify that aviation fuel has been produced from renewable sources and meets sustainability criteria

**2. Agricultural commodity certificates:** certifications that provide assurance that agricultural products have been produced in a sustainable way, enabling either zero-deforestation or other environmental claims.

**3. Industrial commodity certificates:** certifications for materials such as steel, aluminum and cement, which may include verification that these industrial commodities have been produced from sources that have reached a level of emissions performance compatible with Paris-aligned trajectories.

### Carbon Credits (pp. 92–93)

**Avoidance credits:** certificates issued from activities that prevent potential future emissions compared to a counterfactual baseline scenario. The amount of credits eligible for issuance in any given year results from comparing the emissions performance of an activity with the level of emissions in the counterfactual scenario in that year. Example: a greenfield zero- or lower-carbon electricity project may generate carbon credits provided that, in the absence of revenue from the sale of carbon credits, a higher emissions alternative would have been built and operated instead. According to the University of Oxford's Smith School of Enterprise and the Environment, avoided or emission reduction credits represent **over 80% of credits issued in 2023.**

**Emission reduction credits:** certificates issued from activities that reduce GHG emissions compared to emissions in a reference or base year. The amount of credits eligible for issuance in any given year results from comparing the emissions performance of an activity in a given year with the level of emissions in the base year. Examples: energy efficiency measures (e.g. in buildings or industrial processes) or switching from higher-carbon to lower-carbon fuels.

**Carbon removal credits:** originate from carbon removal and storage activities that, according to the Oxford University Net-Zero Aligned Principles, involve "sequestering carbon from the atmosphere and storing it in biological or geological reservoirs." The number of carbon credits eligible for issuance in a given year is determined by measuring the **increase in stored carbon** as a result of the activity, compared to the baseline level of carbon stored before the activity began.
- **Biological carbon sequestration examples:** restoring or enhancing natural carbon stocks or the sequestration of carbon in soil
- **Geological sequestration:** typically involves capturing carbon dioxide directly from the atmosphere and storing it in underground geological reservoirs or through mineral carbonation, where carbon dioxide is converted into stable mineral compounds

*Note (fn. 83): Parties recently agreed to defer discussion on whether emission avoidance could qualify for crediting in Article 6.2 and 6.4 until 2028, while clarifying that emission avoidance is not permitted under Article 8.*

### Models for Chains of Custody and Traceability (p. 94)

**Identity preserved:** most stringent level. Ensures a product's specific attributes and origin are maintained throughout the supply chain; each batch is kept separate from other batches, preserving its unique identity. Products from different sources cannot be physically mixed and must be documented accordingly.

**Physical segregation:** certified product is kept separate from non-certified sources but does not prevent mixing from different certified sources with the same standard. Characteristics maintained from initial input to final output, but no unique identification of the source.

**Mass balance:** certified and non-certified products can be physically mixed, reducing the ability to trace the product back to its origins. A certain volume of certified products enters the supply chain and an equivalent volume leaving operations can be sold as certified. GHG Protocol distinguishes between:
- **Controlled blending:** allows establishment of physical traceability on the certified share of product
- **Mass balance:** does not ensure physical traceability to a specific origin

**Book and claim:** physical products — whether certified or not — are not tracked and flow in the same supply chain. Certificates or credits are traded **separately** according to the amount of certified product fed into the supply chain (i.e. the certificates or credits are "unbundled").

**AIM Platform traceability categories (Advanced Indirect Mitigation platform):**
- **Physical association:** interventions that can be physically traced to specific activities within a company's value chain
- **Close association:** applies to activities with reasonable, albeit lower, levels of traceability (e.g. sourcing from areas where physical segregation of certified and non-certified resources is not feasible)
- **Sector association:** used for interventions that may not be physically traceable to specific activities but can still have a comparable impact due to their relevance to the sector

**Parameters for substantiating value chain mitigation claims through carbon credits:**
- **Quality criteria:** ensures the carbon credit represents mitigation outcomes that are measurable, verifiable, and permanent
- **Degree of association:** both the physical and temporal connection between the activity from which carbon credits originate and the entity making the claim; credits must effectively represent a mitigation outcome traceable to activities and emissions sources within the company's value chain and GHG inventory
- **Fungibility:** compatibility of a carbon credit with a corporate GHG inventory to ensure both instruments can be used in conjunction to substantiate value chain emission reduction and/or removal claims. A credit representing abatement of emissions (mitigation within value chain) may be suitable; a credit representing avoided emissions may not, because it compares actual performance against a counterfactual hypothetical scenario.

**Carbon credits from mitigation activities outside the value chain (p. 95):**

A significant portion of climate-related claims made by companies today are based on carbon credits that originate from activities that **cannot be traced back to the company's value chain**, either physically or temporally.

Two categories of claims:
- **Contribution claims:** communicate that an organization has provided support or financing to actions beyond the company's value chain that are expected to result in climate mitigation outcomes. **Do not suggest** that such support directly reduces the company's environmental impact on the climate.
- **Compensation claims:** imply that purchasing carbon credits is equivalent to reducing emissions within the organizational boundary or value chain of a company. This implied equivalence is the basis for the practice of **offsetting**, whereby a company purchases carbon credits instead of reducing emissions at the source.

**Concerns about offsetting practice:**
- *Supply-side:* whether credits truly represent the mitigation outcomes they convey; permanence of outcomes; various social and environmental aspects
- *Demand-side:* potential **mitigation deterrence** when companies purchase carbon credits instead of abating emissions; potential **finance dilution** due to the differential between the price of carbon credits and the cost of abating emissions; alignment with transition pathways; non-fungibility between GHG inventories and carbon credits; integrity of claims

**Arguments in support of carbon credits:**
- Role that carbon markets can play in channeling **private sector finance** towards mitigation activities
- Role of carbon finance for activities for which **alternative finance mechanisms are lacking**
- Some projects provide **co-benefits** (biodiversity conservation, community development)
- Lower entry barrier for entities to participate in climate action efforts
